# Design Portfolio Case Study — Best Practices

## What Hiring Managers & Design Leads Look For

1. **Clear problem framing** — Can you articulate the problem in one sentence?
2. **Evidence of process** — Did you just make pretty screens, or did you think?
3. **Strategic reasoning** — Why THIS solution over alternatives?
4. **Impact & outcomes** — What changed because of your work?
5. **Self-awareness** — What would you do differently?
6. **Communication clarity** — Is this easy to follow in 5 minutes?

## Patterns from Top Designers

### Simon Pan (Google, Uber)
- Leads with a bold thesis statement about the project
- Heavy emphasis on the problem space before showing any solutions
- Uses data and user quotes to ground decisions
- Clean visual hierarchy — lots of whitespace, strategic image placement
- Ends with measurable impact

### Tiffany Eaton (Spotify, Dropbox)
- Opens with the emotional hook — why does this matter to real people?
- Shows the messy middle — sticky notes, whiteboard sketches, failed iterations
- Pairs every design decision with the insight that drove it
- Concise writing — punchy sentences, no filler
- Reflection section: what she learned, what she'd change

### Mehmet Aydin Bas (Meta, Lyft)
- Strategic framing: positions every project as a business problem first
- Shows competitive analysis and market context
- Detailed interaction design rationale
- Before/after comparisons with clear metrics
- Keeps it scannable with clear section headers

## Universal Principles

### Lead with Impact
Bad: "I was tasked with redesigning the checkout flow."
Good: "A 34% cart abandonment rate was costing the business $2.1M annually. I led the checkout redesign that cut it in half."

### Show Your Thinking, Not Just Your Output
- Include sketches, wireframes, and explorations that DIDN'T make it
- Explain decision points and trade-offs
- Show how user research changed your direction

### Be Specific
Bad: "I conducted user research and found several pain points."
Good: "I interviewed 12 users over 2 weeks. The top pain point (mentioned by 9 of 12): they couldn't tell if their order had actually been placed."

### Keep It Scannable
- Someone should get the gist in 60 seconds by reading only headers and image captions
- Use pull quotes, callout boxes, and bold text for key insights
- Aim for a 5-minute full read

### End Strong
- Quantify impact wherever possible (metrics, adoption, revenue, time saved)
- Include stakeholder or user quotes if available
- Brief reflection: what you learned, what you'd iterate on

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **The screenshot dump** — Lots of pretty screens with no context or thinking
- **The novel** — 3000+ words with no visual breaks. Readers bail.
- **Missing your role** — "We did X" without clarifying YOUR contribution
- **No metrics** — "The project was a success" without evidence
- **Process theater** — Showing a design thinking diamond just because, without genuine process artifacts
- **Burying the lede** — 500 words of background before getting to anything interesting
- **Generic descriptions** — "I iterated based on feedback" (what feedback? what changed?)

## Slide Deck Specific Tips

- **Title slide:** Project name, your role, one-line hook. That's it.
- **One idea per slide** — if you're cramming, split it
- **Visuals do the heavy lifting** — body copy supports, doesn't replace images
- **Speaker notes are conversational** — write what you'd actually say, not a script
- **End with a CTA** — what do you want the audience to take away or do next?
